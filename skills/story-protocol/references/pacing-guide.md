# Story Protocol: Pacing and Narrative Frameworks

## Dan Harmon Story Circle

The 8-step structure that maps character transformation through disruption and return.

```
        YOU
       /    \
     NEED    CHANGE
    /              \
  GO               RETURN
    \              /
     SEARCH   PAY PRICE
       \    /
        FIND
```

### The Eight Steps

1. **YOU** — A character is in a zone of comfort (their ordinary world). Establish who they are and what they have.

2. **NEED** — Something is missing or wrong. The character wants something — consciously or unconsciously. The want creates the vector.

3. **GO** — The character enters an unfamiliar situation. They cross the threshold. In UET-1 stories, this is often: boarding the train, accepting a contract, opening an archive, receiving a device.

4. **SEARCH** — The character adapts to the new situation. They try things. They fail. They learn. This is the longest, most textured section.

5. **FIND** — The character gets what they were looking for — the apparent goal. But getting it changes things.

6. **PAY PRICE** — They pay a heavy price for having found what they wanted. The cost is always personal. The world takes something back.

7. **RETURN** — The character returns to their familiar situation — but they are changed. The familiar world is now strange to them.

8. **CHANGE** — They have changed. The circle closes. What they return with is different from what they left with.

### Story Circle in UET-1 Context

The train enforces the circle structurally:
- Boarding = entering unfamiliar situation (Step 3)
- The journey = search and adaptation (Step 4)
- Arrival or arrest = getting what was wanted / paying price (Steps 5-6)
- Disembarkation or confinement = return (Step 7)

Characters who cannot leave the train cannot complete Steps 7-8 on the train — their return is deferred. This creates dramatic irony: they are changed but have nowhere to return to yet.

### Circle Dial Settings (1-4)

| Dial | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Light touch — story has a natural beginning/middle/end but the circle structure is not emphasized. Steps may blend or be implied. |
| 2 | Standard — all 8 steps present but not labored. Pacing follows the circle naturally. |
| 3 | Pronounced — the circle structure is visible and intentional. Callbacks between Step 1 and Step 8 are explicit. |
| 4 | Full architecture — the story is self-consciously structured on the circle. Each step is a distinct beat. The story may acknowledge its own structure. |

## MICE Quotient (Orson Scott Card)

Four story types that determine what a story is "about" at its core. Stories contain multiple MICE threads, but one dominates — and the dominant frame must be resolved last.

### The Four Threads

**M — Milieu**
- The story is about the WORLD or ENVIRONMENT.
- Opens: Character arrives in a new place or situation
- Closes: Character leaves that place, or the situation fully resolves
- The story is done when we understand the milieu completely enough, or when the character escapes/accepts it
- In UET-1: Milieu stories are about the train itself, the corridor, the surveillance infrastructure

**I — Idea**
- The story is about a QUESTION or THESIS being answered.
- Opens: A question is posed (explicitly or implicitly)
- Closes: The question is answered
- The story is done when the question is resolved — or definitively cannot be resolved
- In UET-1: Idea stories explore questions like "Can a contract replace governance?" or "Does the body belong to its owner?"

**C — Character**
- The story is about TRANSFORMATION of a person.
- Opens: A character is dissatisfied with their role or identity
- Closes: The character either accepts or rejects a new role/identity
- The story is done when the transformation is complete or definitively refused
- In UET-1: Character stories track inner change — the erosion of Rowan's somatic autonomy, Felix's idealism curdling

**E — Event**
- The story is about a DISRUPTION to normal order that must be resolved.
- Opens: The disruption occurs (the attack, the disappearance, the discovery)
- Closes: A new status quo is established (restored or transformed)
- The story is done when the world has reached a new equilibrium
- In UET-1: Event stories are about the aftermath of Black Wednesday, Lin's disappearance, the Stolar extraction

### MICE Nesting Rules

Stories always have multiple MICE threads running simultaneously. The critical rule:

**Nested frames must close in reverse order of opening.**

If you open M → I → C → E, you must close E → C → I → M.

The outermost frame (opened first, closed last) is the story's dominant genre/type.

Example (The Headless Empire structure):
- Outer frame (opened first): **Idea** — "Can innovation be emancipatory?"
- Middle frame: **Event** — Lin Yuan Exact's disappearance
- Inner frame: **Character** — Felix Lim's transformation
- Innermost: **Milieu** — The Covenant as a system

Resolution order: Milieu resolves (Covenant runs autonomously) → Character resolves (Felix reaches new understanding) → Event resolves (Lin resurfaces) → Idea resolves (the arc bends toward capture, not justice)

### MICE Dial Settings (1-4)

| Dial | Description |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Single thread dominant, others minimal. Clean, focused. |
| 2 | Two active threads with clear hierarchy. Main thread drives; secondary enriches. |
| 3 | Three threads active, deliberately layered. Nesting is visible in structure. |
| 4 | All four threads active, fully nested. Each thread opens and closes distinctly. Complex, architectural. |

## Pacing Rhythms

### Scene-Level Pacing

**Slow scenes** (high detail, low momentum):
- Use for: establishing milieu, interior character work, building dread
- Techniques: cataloguing (Rowan listing compounds), bureaucratic enumeration (Murkin's archive), sensory grounding
- Danger: slowing too much breaks tension

**Fast scenes** (low detail, high momentum):
- Use for: action, discovery, transition
- Techniques: short declarative sentences, cutting mid-action, reported action rather than shown
- Danger: speeding too much prevents emotional landing

**Scene transitions on UET-1**:
- Time is marked by gauge changes, jurisdiction crossings, meal service
- Use these as chapter/section breaks
- The train's movement is constant — use it as a background clock

### Tension Types

| Type | Description | UET-1 Expression |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| Surveillance tension | The watched feeling | Bio-band color changes, ECOROUTE alerts, Pono activations |
| Jurisdictional tension | Rules change mid-motion | Approaching a border crossing, detention authority shifts |
| Somatic tension | The body as threat or evidence | Biometric readings, compound effects, physical symptoms |
| Archival tension | The past surfacing | Mirage files, Pono recall, documents that shouldn't exist |
| Relational tension | Estrangement and proximity | Characters who know each other and can't say so |

## Structural Templates

### The Murkin Template (Bureaucratic Absurdism)
Open with procedure → procedure encounters anomaly → procedure attempts to process anomaly → procedure fails gracefully → character makes choice that procedure cannot capture → file report containing "nothing of note"

### The Rowan Template (Technical Self-Implication)
Open with competence → competence used against competent person → recognition arrives too late → body already compromised → sardonic acceptance of new condition

### The Spy/Carrier Template (Parallel Choice)
Two characters in the same situation → neither knows the other is there → each makes the same choice independently → the parallelism is revealed to reader, not characters → the choice is the story

### The Felix Template (Thesis Destruction)
Open with thesis stated sincerely → evidence mounts against thesis → thesis restated with less certainty → evidence becomes undeniable → thesis rewritten into something darker and truer
